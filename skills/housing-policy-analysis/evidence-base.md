# Evidence Base

Peer-reviewed fiscal and planning literature for evaluating claims about density, sprawl, infrastructure costs, and revenue productivity. Use this to ground analysis in evidence rather than vibes.

## Summary

The peer-reviewed literature overwhelmingly finds that compact development costs less to serve and generates more revenue per acre than sprawl. The direction is not in dispute. The magnitude varies by study. The contrarian literature is thin, aging, and largely non-peer-reviewed.

## Peer-Reviewed Evidence

### Meta-Analyses and Literature Reviews (Tier 1)

**Ahlfeldt & Pietrostefani (2017)** — "The Economic Effects of Density: A Synthesis"
- *SERC Discussion Paper 210, London School of Economics*
- 102 studies, 202 elasticity estimates, 15 outcome categories
- Key elasticities per doubling of density:
  - Local public spending: **-14.4%**
  - Wages: **+4%**
  - Rent: **+21%**
  - VMT reduction: **-8.5%**
  - Non-car mode share: **+7%**
  - Crime: **+8.5%**
  - Energy use: **-11%**
- Higher-quality studies (SMS ≥ 3) show ~6% smaller elasticities — effect is real but best studies find slightly more conservative magnitudes
- Critical nuance: density harms renters via scarcity rent (21% rent elasticity). This is an argument for coupling density with tenant protections, not against density.

**Ewing & Hamidi (2015)** — "Compactness versus Sprawl: A Review of Recent Evidence from the United States"
- *Journal of Planning Literature*
- Comprehensive post-1997 review
- Conclusion: "the empirical evidence in favor of compact development is overwhelming"

### National/Regional Studies (Tier 2)

**Burchell et al. (2002)** — TCRP Report 74: "Costs of Sprawl 2000"
- *Transportation Research Board (National Academies)*
- National 25-year scenario modeling, all US counties
- Key findings:
  - Water/sewer savings: **$12.6B**
  - Road savings: **$109.7B** (188,305 fewer lane-miles)
  - Property development savings: **$420B** (6.6%)
  - Annual fiscal improvement: **$4.2B/yr**
- Legitimate critique: assumes linear density-cost relationship (Ladd found U-shaped). Directional finding confirmed by subsequent research.

**Goodman (2019)** — "The Fiscal Impacts of Urban Sprawl: Evidence From U.S. County Areas"
- *Public Budgeting & Finance*, Vol. 39, No. 4, pp. 3-27
- US county panel, 1982-2012 (30 years of data)
- **Critical distinction: spatial extent vs. density**
  - Reducing developed land from 50th to 25th percentile: **-$60.86/capita**
  - Increasing density 25th to 50th percentile: **+$5.26/capita**
  - Ratio: **12:1** — the footprint of development matters far more than density per se
- Implication: a suburb of rowhouses on a compact footprint is nearly as efficient as mid-rise urban

**Carruthers & Ulfarsson (2003)** — "Urban Sprawl and the Cost of Public Services"
- *Environment and Planning B*, Vol. 30, No. 4, pp. 503-522
- 283 US metropolitan counties, 1982-1992
- 1% density increase → **2-4% decrease** in police/education costs
- ~3% decrease across all 12 service categories
- No significant density impact on fire protection or solid waste

### International Studies (Tier 2-3)

**Hortas-Rico & Sole-Olle (2010)** — Spain, 2,500 municipalities
- *Urban Studies*, Vol. 47, No. 7
- Twofold expansion of urbanized land increases community facility costs +11%, police +9%, housing +8%, culture/sports +15%

**Halifax Growth Scenarios (2009)** — 4 scenarios, 2009-2031
- Most compact scenario saves **$3.07B** vs trend, **$715M net** for municipality
- "New residential developments do not pay their way" — but compact ones lose far less

**Sustainable Prosperity (2013)** — Canadian municipalities
- Edmonton: **$4B fiscal loss** over 60 years for 17 of 40 developments
- Calgary: **$11B capital savings** from compact growth
- Kitchener: suburban service charges **74-157% higher**

**Lower Austria (2019)** — Michelhausen, 20-year model
- Low-density (91.5% detached): **-€1.93M at 5 years**, barely breaks even at 20
- Compact (76% row houses): **-€1.05M at 5 years, +€1.82M surplus at 20 years**
- Compact accommodated **60% more inhabitants** on same land

**Vermeiren et al. (2021)** — Flanders, Belgium
- *Land Use Policy*
- Curbing sprawl saves **€246-383M/yr** in infrastructure, **€1.16-1.97B/yr** in mobility costs

### The U-Shaped Density-Cost Curve

**Ladd (1992)** — "Population Growth, Density, and the Costs of Providing Public Services"
- *Urban Studies*, Vol. 29, No. 2
- 247 large US county areas
- Found quadratic (U-shaped) relationship:
  - Cost-minimizing density: **~250 residents/sq mi**
  - Very low-density counties: **14% above** optimal
  - Very high-density counties (24,000+/sq mi): **43% above** optimal
- **Policy relevance:** The ascending right limb (density too high = expensive) applies essentially only to Manhattan. Virtually all US policy decisions — including everything in San Diego — fall on the descending left limb where more density = lower costs.

**IDB Working Paper** — Latin American municipalities
- Optimal spending at ~9,000 residents/sq km (~23,300/sq mi)
- 85% of municipalities in sample were below optimal density

### Revenue-Per-Acre Data (Tier 4)

Practitioner studies, not regression-based, but methodologically transparent:

| Location | Finding |
|----------|---------|
| Nashville | Infill: **1,150x** more net revenue/acre than suburban. Downtown pays for itself in **3 years** vs **42 years** suburban |
| Smart Growth America (17 studies) | Smart growth generates **10x** more tax revenue/acre |
| Denver | Downtown CBD: **$4.3M annual surplus**. Suburban Montbello: **$44.3M infrastructure liability** |
| Asheville, NC | Downtown hotel: **$634K tax/acre**. Walmart: **$6.5K tax/acre** (100:1 ratio) |
| Raleigh, NC | Six-story downtown building: **50x** property tax/acre of average Walmart |

## The Contrarian Literature: Assessed

### Gordon & Richardson (1997) — "Are Compact Cities a Desirable Planning Goal?"
- *JAPA*, Vol. 63, No. 1 — **peer-reviewed**, APA's flagship journal, ~1,200 citations
- Published as deliberate point-counterpoint; Ewing's rebuttal ran in same issue
- Core argument: revealed preference (people chose suburbs → suburbs are optimal)
- **Problems:** Does not account for massive policy distortions (highway subsidies, exclusionary zoning, FHA patterns, parking mandates). Uses commute time as primary metric, ignoring VMT, household costs, externalities. Conflates suburbanization with sprawl.
- **Status:** Real paper, arguments substantially refuted by subsequent 25 years of empirical work

### Wendell Cox (Demographia, Heritage Foundation, Heartland Institute)
- **No peer-reviewed publications** in academic journals
- No tenured/tenure-track academic position
- Affiliations: Heritage Foundation, Heartland Institute, Cato Institute, Reason Foundation
- Demographia Housing Affordability Survey: not peer-reviewed, criticized for using median-multiple without adjusting for housing quality/size/location
- **Status:** Policy advocate, not academic researcher. Citing Cox in academic context is equivalent to citing a lobbyist.

### Cox & Utt (2004) — Heritage Foundation "The Costs of Sprawl Reconsidered"
- **Not peer-reviewed.** Heritage Foundation policy brief
- Central error: compares per-capita municipal spending across municipalities without controlling for service scope, quality, or cost of living
- Litman's point-by-point rebuttal (2015) identifies specific misrepresentations:
  - Cox claims Hsieh & Moretti criticize urban containment; they actually recommend Smart Growth
  - Cox claims Saks criticizes density; she actually criticizes sprawl-inducing regulations
- **Status:** Advocacy document. Thoroughly refuted.

### Glaeser & Kahn (2004) — "Sprawl and Urban Growth"
- *Handbook of Regional and Urban Economics*, Vol. 4 (Elsevier) — prestigious invited chapter
- **Often misrepresented by sprawl defenders.** Actually says:
  - Sprawl is "the inexorable product of car-based living"
  - "The primary social problem associated with sprawl": equity
  - Acknowledges congestion externalities warrant policy intervention
- Glaeser subsequently wrote *Triumph of the City* (2011) explicitly arguing dense cities are greener, more productive, more innovative
- **Status:** Citing Glaeser as pro-sprawl is a misrepresentation of his work

## Where Genuine Uncertainty Exists

1. **Magnitude, not direction.** Effect sizes vary. The direction is not in question.
2. **The U-shaped curve is real** but the right limb applies only above ~24,000/sq mi. Irrelevant for any US city outside Manhattan.
3. **Density harms renters through scarcity rent** (21% elasticity). This is an argument for coupling density with tenant protections, not against density.
4. **Spatial extent matters 12x more than density** (Goodman 2019). A compact suburb of rowhouses is nearly as efficient as mid-rise urban. The real fiscal killer is spreading low-density development over vast infrastructure.
5. **Supply constraints raise prices** (Glaeser). But the solution is removing barriers to infill density, not encouraging greenfield sprawl.

## Rebuttal Quick Reference

| Claim | Rebuttal | Key Citation |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| "People chose suburbs" | Distorted menu: highway subsidies, exclusionary zoning, FHA, parking mandates | Gordon & Richardson refuted by Ewing 1997, subsequent 25 years |
| "Density is expensive" | Only above ~24,000/sq mi. All US policy decisions are on descending left limb | Ladd 1992 |
| "New development pays for itself" | For 20-30 years. Replacement cycle at 30-50 years reveals true cost | Halifax 2009, Lower Austria 2019 |
| "Sprawl is efficient" | 10-100x less revenue/acre, higher per-capita infrastructure costs | Revenue-per-acre data, Carruthers & Ulfarsson 2003 |
| "Glaeser supports sprawl" | Misrepresentation. He calls sprawl an equity problem, later wrote pro-density book | Glaeser & Kahn 2004, *Triumph of the City* 2011 |
| "Compact cities are an ideology" | 102 studies, 202 elasticity estimates. This is empirical, not ideological | Ahlfeldt & Pietrostefani 2017 |
| "Infrastructure costs are fixed" | Spatial extent drives 12x more cost variation than density | Goodman 2019 |
