## 2. STATE LAW FLAGS

### 🚨 CRITICAL — Item 21: SB 79 Phasing Ordinance

**Quoted language from agenda:**
> "Introduce a phasing ordinance, as authorized under SB 79, to exclude and exempt eligible sites, and defer implementation of SB 79 on qualifying sites, as authorized under Government Code Sections 65912.157(h), 65912.160(e)(1) and 65912.161(b)(1)."

**The problem:** This is the city's formal move to opt out of SB 79 transit-corridor density mandates using the statute's own phasing provisions. SB 79 does include limited phasing mechanisms — but the city of Oceanside has been simultaneously pursuing a **rushed "alternative plan"** that advocates have flagged as lawsuit bait. Using the phasing ordinance as a delay tactic while that alternative is being developed compounds the risk.

**Key questions staff is NOT answering in this agenda:**
- Which specific sites are being "excluded and exempted," and why?
- What is the timeline for "deferred implementation"? Indefinite deferral is not phasing — it's avoidance.
- Does the phasing ordinance comply with the *mandatory* affordability and density thresholds SB 79 requires even for phased sites?
- Has HCD been notified or consulted on the ordinance language?
- Does this interact with the pending YIMBY Law HAA litigation threat (flagged Jan 2025)? Sites excluded under this ordinance may still be subject to Builder's Remedy if Oceanside's housing element compliance is contested.

**State law at risk:** Gov. Code §§ 65912.157, 65912.160, 65912.161 (SB 79 phasing provisions — narrow exceptions, not blanket deferrals). If the ordinance exceeds the statutory phasing authority, it becomes an illegal downzone potentially violating SB 330 § 66300(b)(1) (no reduction in residential intensity below Jan. 1, 2018 baseline).

**Also watch:** If any excluded sites had SB 330 preliminary applications submitted before this ordinance is adopted, those applications may have **vested rights** that the phasing ordinance cannot extinguish. Records request needed immediately (see Action Items).

### ⚠️ MODERATE — Item 19: Alley Vacation, Block 75, Horne's Addition

**Quoted language:**
> "Ordering the vacation of the Alley located in Block 75 of Horne's Addition Map No. 323"

**Flag:** Alley vacations in urban infill areas can eliminate rear-parcel access needed for ADU construction, lot splits (SB 9), or future infill development. The agenda provides zero information about:
- What parcels are affected
- Whether any active development applications depend on this alley for access
- Whether any ADU applications have been submitted for adjacent properties
- Whether this vacation serves a private landowner's interest at the expense of future housing potential

**State law at risk:** If the vacation eliminates legal access required for a by-right ADU or SB 9 lot split application, it could constitute an illegal constraint under ADU law (Gov. Code § 65852.2) or a constructive denial under HAA § 65589.5(h)(6). **This needs scrutiny before adoption.**

### ✅ CLEAN (with one note) — Item 20: 801-815 Mission Avenue (230 units + density bonus)

**Quoted language:**
> "230 residential units, including 23 units reserved for low-income households, and 5,240 square feet of commercial floor area on a 1.5-acre site located at 801-815 Mission Avenue"

This is a **density bonus project** on Mission Avenue — exactly the kind of infill mixed-use the framework supports. 23 low-income units = 10% affordability, which triggers density bonus eligibility under Gov. Code § 65915.

**One note to verify:** At 230 units on 1.5 acres, that's roughly 153 units/acre — confirm the density bonus was correctly calculated from the base zoning, that no concessions were improperly denied, and that the CDC (not City Council acting as planning body) has jurisdiction here. The CDC acting as approving body is worth confirming doesn't introduce a procedural vulnerability.

**CEQA:** Class 32 Categorical Infill Exemption is the correct exemption for urban infill. Appropriate.

## 5. STRONG TOWNS ASSESSMENT

The fiscal picture this meeting is **mixed but trending negative.** The 801-815 Mission Avenue approval — 230 units at 153 units/acre on 1.5 acres with ground-floor commercial — is a **textbook Strong Towns win**: high tax-value-per-acre, walkable Mission Avenue corridor, minimal new infrastructure required, infill not sprawl. This is the kind of incremental intensification that builds long-term fiscal productivity. Contrast this with the SB 79 phasing ordinance, which — if it excludes transit-corridor sites from upzoning — effectively locks in car-dependent, low-density land use patterns near transit, guaranteeing lower tax productivity per acre on those sites for years and increasing the city's long-term infrastructure cost-per-resident. The city's ongoing $160M+ water/sewer spending is not addressed this meeting, but the SB 79 deferrals directly undermine any fiscal justification for that infrastructure investment: you don't spend $160M extending capacity and then refuse to allow the density that makes that investment productive. The alley vacation (Item 19) carries a similar micro-scale risk — vacating urban alleys reduces fine-grained infill potential, which is precisely the kind of incremental development Strong Towns identifies as most fiscally productive.
