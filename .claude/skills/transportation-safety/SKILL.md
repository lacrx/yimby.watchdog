# Transportation Safety Analysis

Framework for evaluating transportation safety claims against proper epidemiological and engineering standards. Companion to policy-analysis (land-use framing) — this skill handles the specific domain of active transportation infrastructure, crash data, and the manipulation patterns used to justify car-centric decisions.

## Core Principle

Transportation safety is an empirical question with a large peer-reviewed evidence base. Treat it like one. When someone claims protected bike infrastructure is dangerous, apply the same scrutiny you'd apply to someone claiming vaccines cause autism — check the methodology, check the credentials, check whether the claim is consistent with the global evidence base, and check who benefits from the conclusion.

## Step 1. Evaluate the Claim Source

### Evidence Hierarchy (Transportation Safety)

1. **Peer-reviewed meta-analyses and systematic reviews**
   - Teschke et al. 2012 (*BMJ*): 14 route types compared; cycle tracks = 9x safer than major streets with parked cars
   - Marshall & Ferenchak 2019 (*Journal of Transport & Health*): 12 US cities, 13 years — cities with more protected bike infrastructure had 44% fewer fatalities, 50% fewer serious injuries across ALL road users
   - Reynolds et al. 2009 (*Environmental Health Perspectives*): first systematic review of cycling injury risk by facility type; roundabouts and multi-use paths at intersections = higher risk; cycle tracks = lower risk

2. **Peer-reviewed empirical studies with large samples**
   - Cicchino et al. 2020 (*Journal of Safety Research*): IIHS study, 12 cities, found protected bike lanes associated with 44% lower odds of injury
   - Lusk et al. 2011 (*Injury Prevention*): Montreal cycle tracks, 6 years — 28% lower injury rate than parallel reference streets
   - Harris et al. 2013 (*American Journal of Public Health*): Vancouver, 14 route types — cycle tracks = lowest injury risk
   - DiGioia et al. 2017 (*Accident Analysis & Prevention*): meta-analysis of CMFs for bike facilities

3. **Government research reports with expert review panels**
   - FHWA-HRT-23-078 (Dixon et al. 2023): CMF of 0.47 for flex-post SBLs (53% crash reduction, mid-block segments only). Limitations: cross-sectional design, no intersection data, modeled bike counts. Still the largest US-specific SBL safety dataset.
   - FHWA 2015 *Separated Bike Lane Planning and Design Guide*: inconclusive on overall safety but recommended better data collection
   - NACTO design guidance
   - AASHTO *Guide for the Development of Bicycle Facilities*

4. **Municipal crash data analyses with transparent methodology**
   - Before-after studies with ridership denominators, statistical testing, control corridors
   - Must have: exposure data, confidence intervals, confounder controls

5. **Self-published reports, advocacy papers, blog posts, council presentations**
   - Treat as claims requiring verification, not evidence
   - Check: peer review status, author credentials in transportation, funding sources, ideological commitments, whether conclusions match data

### Author Credential Check

Transportation safety is an interdisciplinary field (civil engineering, public health epidemiology, urban planning). Legitimate credentials include:
- Faculty appointment in transportation engineering, public health, or urban planning
- Publication record in transportation safety journals (*Accident Analysis & Prevention*, *Transportation Research Record*, *Journal of Safety Research*, *Journal of Transport & Health*, *Injury Prevention*)
- Professional engineer (PE) license in transportation/traffic engineering
- Research affiliation with recognized safety centers (IIHS, SafeTREC, VTTI, HSRC)

**Red flags:**
- PhD in unrelated field (e.g., cancer biology) presented as transportation expertise
- No peer-reviewed publications in transportation safety
- "Former traffic commissioner" (volunteer advisory role, not professional qualification)
- Credentials in clinical medicine (MD) without epidemiology or public health training
- League of American Bicyclists Certified Instructor (cycling advocacy certification, not research credential)

## Step 2. Evaluate the Methodology

### The Crash Data Checklist

When evaluating ANY crash study or safety claim, check every item:

**[ ] Denominator / Exposure**
- Does it report crash RATES (per trip, per mile, per rider) or raw COUNTS?
- Counts without denominators are meaningless for safety assessment
- Protected infrastructure is designed to increase ridership — more riders = more total crashes even if per-rider risk drops
- The question is never "did crashes increase?" but "did the crash RATE change?"
- If no ridership data exists, the study cannot make safety conclusions. Period.

**[ ] Statistical Testing**
- Is there a test of statistical significance? (chi-square, Fisher's exact, Poisson regression)
- Are confidence intervals reported?
- What is the sample size? (N < 50 crashes = underpowered for most designs)
- Was multiple testing corrected for?
- If no statistical test is applied, the reported percentage change is anecdotal

**[ ] Study Design**
- Before-after (longitudinal): preferred — same street, before and after intervention
- Cross-sectional: weaker — different streets compared at same time, more confounders
- Case report (N=1 corridor): hypothesis-generating only, cannot support general conclusions
- Ecological study (city-level aggregates): can show association, cannot prove causation for specific interventions

**[ ] Data Source Integrity**
- Are crash sources clearly identified? (SWITRS/TIMS, CCRS, police reports, EMS logs, hospital records)
- Are sources consistent between comparison periods?
- Is crowdsourced/self-reported data mixed with official data?
- If mixing official and crowdsourced: are they separated in analysis? Is crowdsourced data available for BOTH comparison periods?
- Does the study acknowledge police report under-capture? (Literature: 40-92% of bicycle crashes missing from police reports)

**[ ] Confounders Controlled**
- Ridership/exposure changes (e-bike adoption, COVID, seasonal patterns)
- Vehicle speeds and volumes
- Road geometry changes
- Intersection density and configuration
- Demographics of road users (experienced vs. casual, age distribution)
- E-bike proliferation (heavier, faster, less experienced riders — massive confounder post-2019)
- Weather, lighting, construction activity
- Changes in crash reporting methodology

**[ ] Crash Type Accounting**
- Does it count ALL crash types or only a subset?
- Vehicle-bicycle, solo bicycle, bicycle-pedestrian, bicycle-bicycle, fixed object?
- Intersection AND mid-block?
- If only mid-block vehicle-bicycle crashes: the study excludes the majority of bicycle crashes
- Counting only infrastructure-caused crashes (hitting bollards) without comparing to car-caused crashes (being hit by vehicles) is one-sided accounting

**[ ] Control Comparison**
- Is there a control corridor/period?
- Was the control modified during the study period?
- Are control and treatment corridors genuinely comparable? (same speed, volume, land use, intersection density)

### The Crowdsourced Data Problem

Crowdsourced crash data (Facebook groups, email lists, self-reports from victims) has structural biases:

1. **Reporting bias**: people who oppose infrastructure are more likely to report crashes attributed to it
2. **Recall bias**: self-reported severity is unreliable
3. **Selection bias**: only post-installation data exists (nobody was crowdsourcing before)
4. **Verification**: no independent verification standard for most crowdsourced lists
5. **Conflict of interest**: when the data collector is also an advocate for removal, the data chain is compromised

Crowdsourced data CAN supplement official records — but it cannot be mixed with official data in before-after comparisons unless the same crowdsourcing methodology was applied in both periods. Otherwise, it systematically inflates the "after" count.

**EMS dispatch logs** are a legitimate supplementary source — they're official, systematic, and collected consistently across time periods. Combining CCRS + EMS is defensible methodology. Combining CCRS + EMS + Facebook is not.

## Step 3. What the Evidence Actually Shows

### Protected Bike Infrastructure — Global Evidence Base

The academic literature is clear and directionally consistent:

- **Protected bike lanes reduce injuries and fatalities.** Every meta-analysis and systematic review finds this. The magnitude varies by study design and context, but the direction does not.
- **No protected bike lane in peer-reviewed literature has produced a net increase in crashes.** Individual corridors may see increases in certain crash types (especially intersection right hooks), but overall corridor safety improves.
- **The safety benefit compounds with network effects.** Isolated segments connected to nothing perform worse than connected networks. Safety-in-numbers: more cyclists = lower per-capita crash rate.
- **Design matters enormously.** Flex posts ≠ curb-separated ≠ parking-protected ≠ grade-separated. Width, intersection treatment, sight lines, mixing zone design, and maintenance all affect outcomes.
- **The comparison baseline is "mixing with car traffic," which kills people.** Any evaluation of protected infrastructure must compare to the counterfactual — not to a theoretical zero-risk environment.

### What Legitimate Concerns Exist

- **Intersection right hooks** are a real design problem for protected lanes, especially where vertical elements extend to intersections without adequate mixing zones. Multiple fatalities documented. This is a design problem, not an argument against the category.
- **Narrow protected lanes** (< 7 ft rideable width) create speed differential hazards when e-bikes, road cyclists, and casual riders share space.
- **Wheel stops and bollards** create fixed-object collision hazards, especially for cyclists unfamiliar with the corridor. Design should minimize fixed-object hazards while maintaining separation.
- **Maintenance** — debris accumulation in protected lanes can force cyclists into vehicle lanes or into fixed objects.
- **Police report under-capture** is real and important. 40-92% of bicycle crashes don't appear in police reports. Studies relying solely on CCRS/SWITRS undercount crashes across ALL facility types, not just protected lanes.

These are arguments for **better design and maintenance**, not for removal.

## Step 4. Common Manipulation Patterns

### Pattern: Methodological Laundering

**How it works:** Mix unverified data (social media reports, Facebook groups) with official data (SWITRS/TIMS/CCRS), present through government channels (traffic commission) as if methodology were sound, then export to other jurisdictions as precedent.

**Example:** Encinitas — Commissioner combined CCRS police reports + EMS logs + Facebook group reports into single crash figure, presented as "combined crash data from state and local sources." Cited as "400% increase in serious crashes." SDBC rebuttal: "inaccurate at best and dishonestly calculated at worst."

**How to break it:** Demand data source separation. Which crashes are CCRS? Which are EMS? Which are crowdsourced? Are comparison periods using the same sources?

### Pattern: Counts Without Rates

**How it works:** Report raw crash counts increasing after infrastructure installation. Ignore ridership changes. A corridor that went from 500 to 2,000 daily riders with crashes going from 10 to 15 actually got SAFER (rate dropped from 2.0% to 0.75%), but "50% more crashes" sounds alarming.

**How to break it:** Demand the denominator. No ridership data = no safety conclusion possible.

### Pattern: Selective Crash Typing

**How it works:** Count only crashes caused by new infrastructure (hitting bollards, wheel stops) while ignoring the crashes it prevents (rear-end, sideswipe, right hook from mixing with traffic). Like counting seatbelt bruises without counting lives saved.

**How to break it:** Demand full crash-type accounting. All types, all severities, all locations along the corridor.

### Pattern: N=1 Generalization

**How it works:** Study one corridor, find (or claim) an increase, generalize to "separated bikeways are dangerous" as a category. One patient's adverse drug reaction doesn't invalidate a Phase III trial with 10,000 subjects.

**How to break it:** Point to the meta-analyses. Individual corridor experience can indicate design problems; it cannot overturn the global evidence base.

### Pattern: Credentialed Authority Substitution

**How it works:** Present non-transportation credentials (MD, PhD in unrelated field, "traffic commissioner") as if they confer expertise in transportation safety epidemiology. A cancer biologist publishing on bike lane safety is like a traffic engineer publishing on tumor suppression.

**How to break it:** Check publication record in transportation safety journals. Zero peer-reviewed publications = zero standing to overturn the evidence base.

### Pattern: Vehicular Cycling Ideology as Analysis

**How it works:** Start from the ideological position that cyclists should mix with car traffic (vehicular cycling, John Forester). Collect data that supports this conclusion. Recommend removal of physical separation. Never disclose the ideological commitment.

**Identifiers:**
- Recommendation to replace Class IV (protected) with Class II (paint-only)
- Language: "experienced cyclists," "competent cyclists," emphasis on cyclist behavior over infrastructure
- Affiliation with CyclingSavvy, League of American Bicyclists instructor certification
- Emphasis on solo bicycle crashes (hitting infrastructure) while minimizing vehicle-caused crashes
- Dismissal of "perceived safety" as illegitimate while claiming "actual safety" requires mixing with traffic

**How to break it:** Vehicular cycling is a discredited theory. Strong Towns, BicycleDutch, PeopleForBikes, NACTO, FHWA, and essentially every transportation safety researcher has rejected it. The theory predicts that mixing with traffic is safer; the data shows the opposite. Require disclosure of VC ideology as a competing interest.

### Pattern: The Regional Export

**How it works:** Produce questionable analysis in one city, then present it at neighboring city councils to justify identical policy (infrastructure removal) across the region. Gives each city political cover ("Encinitas found the same thing").

**Example:** Vista Mayor Franklin invited Encinitas Commissioner Medak to present at Vista council meeting. Fabricated Encinitas data became justification for Vista's bike lane removal.

**How to break it:** Each city must evaluate independently. Ask: is the presenting person a credentialed researcher or a political advocate? Was the data peer-reviewed? Does it apply to this city's specific corridor?

### Pattern: Financial Damage Spiral

**How it works:** Build infrastructure → generate opposition using manipulated data → spend public money removing it → spend more money redesigning → net result: millions spent, zero infrastructure, established precedent against future attempts.

**Track the money:** Installation cost + removal cost + redesign cost + opportunity cost of what the money could have funded instead.

## Step 5. Data Sources and Verification

### Official Crash Data (California)

- **SWITRS/TIMS** (Statewide Integrated Traffic Records System / Transportation Injury Mapping System): UC Berkeley SafeTREC. Geo-coded, verified, includes severity. Gold standard for California. Now called CCRS (California Crash Reporting System) on California Open Data Portal.
- **CCRS**: Same data, newer portal. `data.ca.gov/dataset/ccrs`. Three CSV files per year: Crashes, Parties, InjuredWitnessPassengers. Linked by CollisionId.
- **EMS dispatch logs**: Available via public records request from local fire departments. Systematic, timestamped, severity-triaged. Legitimate supplementary source.
- **Hospital/ED records**: Best for capturing non-vehicle crashes. Available via OSHPD/HCAI databases. IRB-level access restrictions.

### Ridership / Exposure Data

- **SANDAG Open Data Portal**: Permanent counters (Eco Counter Zelt Evo) at select locations. Limited spatial coverage.
- **Strava Metro**: Sample bias toward recreational/athletic riders. Not representative.
- **Manual counts**: Short-duration, seasonal variation, expensive. NBPDP methodology.
- **If no ridership data exists for the study corridor, the study cannot calculate crash rates and cannot make safety conclusions.**

## Rebuttal Templates

### "Protected bike lanes cause more crashes"
The global peer-reviewed evidence base — meta-analyses across hundreds of corridors and dozens of cities — consistently shows protected infrastructure reduces injuries and fatalities. An N=1 case report from authors with no transportation safety publications cannot overturn this. If crashes increased on a specific corridor, investigate the design (sight lines, intersection treatment, width, maintenance) — don't condemn the category.

### "Cyclists are safer mixing with traffic"
This is vehicular cycling theory, originated by John Forester in the 1970s. It has been tested and refuted by 40+ years of empirical evidence. Teschke et al. (2012) found cycle tracks 9x safer than major streets. Marshall & Ferenchak (2019) found cities with more protected infrastructure had 44% fewer fatalities. The theory predicts the opposite of what the data shows.

### "The FHWA study is flawed, so we shouldn't build protected lanes"
Every study has limitations. The FHWA study's limitations (cross-sectional design, modeled bike counts, intersection crashes excluded) are real. But: (a) it's still the largest US-specific dataset, (b) its direction matches every other study in the evidence base, (c) critiquing one study doesn't invalidate the rest of the literature, (d) the critics demanding better methodology should apply the same standards to their own work.

### "We should wait for better data before building"
The default condition — unprotected cyclists mixing with cars — kills people now. Four pedestrians/cyclists died in Encinitas in the first four months of 2025, all killed by cars. The question is not "is the evidence perfect?" but "is protected infrastructure better than the status quo?" The evidence overwhelmingly says yes.

### "Crashes with bollards/wheel stops prove the infrastructure is dangerous"
Solo crashes with fixed objects exist on every road with curbs, light poles, parked cars, and medians. The relevant comparison is: how many people would be hit by cars WITHOUT the bollards? If a corridor has 5 bollard crashes and would have had 20 car-bicycle crashes without separation, the infrastructure saved 15 people from worse outcomes. One-sided accounting (counting only infrastructure-caused crashes) is methodologically invalid.

### "This data shows a 400% increase"
Check: (a) is that a count or a rate? (b) what's the denominator? (c) are the same data sources used in both periods? (d) is there a statistical test? (e) who compiled the data and what is their ideological position? (f) has this been peer-reviewed? If any of these fail, the number is not evidence.
