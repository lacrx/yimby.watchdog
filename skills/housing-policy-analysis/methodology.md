# Policy Analysis Methodology

A 5-step framework for evaluating what land-use policy actually does versus what proponents claim it does.

## Step 1. Ignore the Framing

Traditional land-use debate is fought on vibes. These phrases are marketing — skip them entirely:

- "Neighborhood character"
- "Community input"
- "Responsible growth"
- "Protecting quality of life"
- "Preserving our way of life"

Instead, ask four questions:

| Question | What You're Looking For |
|----------|----------------------|
| What does this policy **physically produce** on the ground? | Units, lane-miles, pipe-feet, service calls, permits issued |
| What does it **cost** to build and maintain? Over what lifecycle? | Capital cost, 30-50 year replacement cost, annual O&M |
| What **revenue** does it generate per acre? | Property tax, sales tax, utility fees — per acre, not per capita |
| **Who pays** and **who benefits**? | Cross-subsidies, infrastructure cost allocation, tax increment flow |

## Step 2. Go to the Actual Data

Start with peer-reviewed literature. Not think tanks, not advocacy orgs, not pop-urbanism.

### Evidence Hierarchy

Use this to weight sources. Higher tiers override lower tiers when they conflict.

| Tier | Source Type | Example | Weight |
|------|-----------|---------|--------|
| 1 | Peer-reviewed meta-analyses and literature reviews | Ahlfeldt & Pietrostefani 2017 (102 studies, 202 elasticity estimates) | Highest |
| 2 | Peer-reviewed empirical studies with large samples | Goodman 2019 (30-year US county panel); Carruthers & Ulfarsson 2003 (283 metro counties) |  |
| 3 | Government-commissioned research with panel review | TCRP Report 74; Halifax Growth Scenarios |  |
| 4 | Practitioner fiscal analyses with transparent math | Revenue-per-acre calculations, IBA reports |  |
| 5 | Think-tank reports from ideologically committed orgs | Heritage Foundation, Cato, Reason — treat as advocacy, verify independently | Lowest |

### Evaluating Contrarian Work

When contrarian literature exists, check:
- Is it peer-reviewed?
- Who funded it?
- Has it been specifically rebutted?
- Has subsequent research confirmed or refuted it?

One paper from 1997 does not outweigh 25 years of replicating studies. A policy advocate with no journal publications is not a counterweight to a meta-analysis.

See [evidence-base.md](evidence-base.md) for the full literature review with contrarian work assessed.

## Step 3. Form a Position From the Evidence

Once you have the data, state what it shows. Plainly. No hedging.

### What the Fiscal Literature Shows

These findings are consistent across the peer-reviewed literature (see [evidence-base.md](evidence-base.md) for citations):

- Compact development generates 10-100x more revenue per acre than sprawl
- Suburban infrastructure costs more to serve per acre than it generates in revenue
- Urban cores cross-subsidize suburban areas, not the other way around
- New development looks profitable for 20-30 years; the replacement cycle at 30-50 years reveals the true cost
- The direction is not in dispute; the magnitude varies by study
- The U-shaped density-cost curve is real but the ascending right limb kicks in at densities essentially no US city outside Manhattan reaches (~24,000/sq mi)

### What the Political Structure Shows

- In cities with district-based representation, suburban districts often outnumber urban ones
- The beneficiaries of cross-subsidy hold the votes to maintain it
- "Neighborhood character" protection and anti-density votes are revealed preferences for maintaining subsidized infrastructure
- State preemption (SB 9, SB 79, Builder's Remedy, HCD enforcement) exists because local political capture makes reform from within structurally impossible

## Step 4. Evaluate Political Actions Against Stated Goals

This is where the framework earns its keep. Take the politician's or agency's **stated goal**, then evaluate their **actual vote or action** against the evidence.

### The Method

1. What did they **say** they want? (affordable housing, fiscal sustainability, safe streets, Vision Zero)
2. What did they actually **vote for / implement / cut**?
3. Does the action **advance** the stated goal according to the evidence?
4. If not, **who actually benefits** from the action?

### Worked Examples

| Stated Goal | Actual Action | Evidence Says | Who Actually Benefits |
|-------------|---------------|---------------|----------------------|
| "Housing affordability" | Cap density, restrict ADU programs | Restricting supply raises prices (Glaeser et al.) | Incumbent homeowners — property values protected |
| "Vision Zero" | Eliminate complete streets engineers | Complete streets reduce pedestrian/cyclist fatalities | Drivers in suburban districts — road maintenance budget preserved |
| "Fiscal sustainability" | Maintain sprawl infrastructure while cutting urban services | Sprawl is a net fiscal drain per the literature | Suburban homeowners — subsidized infrastructure maintained |
| "Community access" | Eliminate paid parking at cultural institutions | Institutions lose arts funding; attendance was already declining | Single-family homeowners — free parking preserved, costs socialized |
| "SB 79 compliance" | Classify major transit hub as lower tier; define "walking path" as continuous sidewalk | Exclude parcels from housing mandate using city's own infrastructure neglect | Existing property owners near transit — development blocked |
| "Protecting renters" | Classify Good Cause eviction as "rent control" for SB 79 exemption | Good Cause eviction ≠ rent stabilization under Costa-Hawkins | Landlords — properties exempted from upzoning |

### The Reveal

In each case, the **stated beneficiary** (renters, pedestrians, the public) is different from the **actual beneficiary** (incumbent property owners, drivers, landlords). This gap between rhetoric and outcome is the core finding of the analysis. Name it explicitly.

## Step 5. Rebut Where Necessary

When the stated rationale doesn't match the evidence, say so directly. Not "there are concerns" — state the contradiction.

### Common False Claims and Evidence-Based Rebuttals

**"People chose suburbs, so suburbs are optimal."**
→ Revealed preference argument ignores massive policy distortions: highway subsidies, exclusionary zoning, parking mandates, FHA lending patterns, property tax structures. People chose from a distorted menu. That doesn't validate the menu.

**"Density is expensive to serve."**
→ True only above ~24,000 people/sq mi (Manhattan). For all practical US policy decisions, moving toward density *reduces* per-capita costs. The relevant range is entirely on the descending left limb of the U-shaped curve. See Ladd 1992.

**"We can't afford transit / complete streets."**
→ You can't afford not to. Car-dependent infrastructure is the fiscal drain. Complete streets engineers generate more long-term fiscal value than the suburban road maintenance they'd be redirected to.

**"Paid parking hurts access."**
→ Free parking is a subsidy. When the subsidy is removed, the question isn't "did attendance drop?" — it's "what's the baseline trend, and what's the counterfactual?" If arts funding gets cut instead, that's the real access harm.

**"This reflects community input."**
→ Community input in land-use is structurally captured by incumbent property owners who show up to meetings. Renters, future residents, and people priced out of the jurisdiction don't testify. "Community input" means "current homeowner preference."

**"The walking path doesn't exist."**
→ The city chose not to build sidewalks, then uses that choice to exclude parcels from state housing mandates. Infrastructure neglect is not a basis for exemption from state law.

**"We need to protect our neighborhood's character."**
→ "Character" in this context is a proxy for income homogeneity and development restriction. Actual character — income diversity, walkable amenities, pedestrian safety — is better preserved by allowing density than by restricting it, because restriction drives up housing costs and displaces the income diversity that constitutes the character.

## Output Format

When applying this framework, structure your analysis as:

1. **The claim** — what the policy/politician says
2. **The action** — what actually happened (vote, ordinance, budget item)
3. **The evidence** — what peer-reviewed literature says about the action's likely effect
4. **The gap** — the contradiction between claim and evidence
5. **The beneficiary** — who actually benefits from the action
6. **The legal exposure** — what state law says about the action (reference [ca-housing-law.md](ca-housing-law.md))
