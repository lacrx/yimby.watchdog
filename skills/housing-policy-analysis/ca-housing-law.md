# California Housing Law Reference

Statute reference, enforcement pipeline, and penalty structure for evaluating municipal compliance with state housing mandates.

## Statute Quick Reference

| Law | Code Section | Core Requirement | Penalty for Violation |
|-----|-------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| **Housing Accountability Act (HAA)** | Gov. Code § 65589.5 | Cannot deny compliant housing without written findings based on objective standards | $10,000/unit minimum; 5x bad faith multiplier; mandatory attorney's fees |
| **SB 330 (Housing Crisis Act)** | Gov. Code § 66300 | No downzoning below Jan 1, 2018 levels; max 5 hearings on compliant projects | HAA penalties apply; extends through 2030 |
| **SB 35 (Streamlining)** | Gov. Code § 65913.4 | Ministerial approval for compliant projects in cities behind RHNA | No discretionary review; CEQA exempt |
| **SB 79 (Transit Density)** | Effective July 1, 2026 | Tier 1: 120 du/ac, 75ft adjacent; Tier 2: 100 du/ac, 65ft within ¼mi | HAA presumption of violation in high-resource areas (Jan 1, 2027) |
| **Density Bonus Law** | Gov. Code § 65915 | Must grant density bonus + concessions/waivers for projects with affordable units | HAA penalties for denial |
| **Housing Element Law** | Gov. Code §§ 65580-65589.9 | Adequate sites, constraint removal, AFFH, HCD certification | Decertification → Builder's Remedy; AB 1398 rezoning deadlines |
| **Builder's Remedy** | Gov. Code § 65589.5(d)(5) | When housing element noncompliant: city cannot deny projects with ≥20% affordable | Applies automatically; vests at preliminary application |
| **ADU Law** | Gov. Code §§ 65852.2, 66310-66342 | Must approve compliant ADUs ministerially | HCD enforcement; AG referral |
| **Coastal Act** | Pub. Resources Code §§ 30000-30900 | CCC retains discretionary authority over coastal development | Can override density bonus waivers on coastal resource grounds |

## SB 79 Tier Classification

This is the most common dispute point in current enforcement. Get it right.

### Tier 1 (Highest Density)
- Very high-frequency commuter rail: ≥72 trains/day across both directions
- Heavy rail (BART, LA Metro heavy rail)
- **Density allowed:** 120 du/ac, 75ft height within ¼ mile; 100 du/ac, 65ft height ¼-½ mile
- **Adjacent intensifier:** +20ft height, +40 du/ac, +1.0 FAR

### Tier 2
- High-frequency commuter rail: ≥48 trains/day
- Light rail (SD Trolley, SF Muni Metro, VTA, SacRT)
- Qualifying bus service: full-time dedicated bus lanes + ≤15 min peak frequency
- **Density allowed:** 100 du/ac, 65ft height within ¼ mile; 80 du/ac, 55ft height ¼-½ mile
- **Adjacent intensifier:** +20ft height, +40 du/ac, +1.0 FAR

### Train Counting Rules (HCD March 20, 2026 MPO Advisory)

Count ALL trains from ALL commuter rail operators serving the station, across both directions. Multiple services at one station are aggregated. This "reflects the overall level of transit access and frequency experienced by riders."

**Red flag:** A city classifying a station with 130+ daily trains as Tier 2. Count ALL operators independently — Amtrak, Metrolink, Coaster, SPRINTER don't coordinate responses, which makes independent verification straightforward.

### "Dedicated Bus Lane" — What Qualifies

SB 79 cross-references only paragraph (1) of Pub. Resources Code § 21060.2(a) — the dedicated-lane + frequency prong. It does NOT import the other 4 BRT requirements (signal priority, all-door boarding, fare collection, defined stations).

**Physical exclusivity is NOT required:**
- SB 79 explicitly requires physical exclusivity for heavy rail (Gov. Code § 65912.156(d): "separate rights-of-way from which all other vehicular and foot traffic are excluded") but conspicuously omits this from the bus lane standard (*expressio unius est exclusio alterius*)
- Vehicle Code § 21655.1(b): right turns into transit-only lanes are expressly permitted
- Vehicle Code § 21655.1(c)(1): local authority may co-designate transit-only lanes for bicycles
- AB 971 (Lee, 2023): passed 77-0 Assembly, 39-0 Senate — unanimously affirmed co-use authorization
- SANDAG 2021 Regional Plan Glossary: "dedicated lanes" include those "designated by signs and markings"

Cities cannot exclude bus corridors from Tier 2 by arguing that bicycle co-use or vehicle right turns negate "dedicated" status.

### SANDAG TOD Stop Map

SANDAG's map carries rebuttable presumption of validity (Gov. Code § 65912.160(f)). Cities relying on underinclusive maps that deny qualifying projects near excluded stops face direct HAA liability beginning Jan 1, 2027 (§ 65912.157(m)).

### Qualifying Criteria for SB 79

A project must:
- Be in a county with ≥16 passenger rail stations
- Be within ½ mile of a TOD stop (¼ mile in cities <35,000)
- Include ≥5 residential units
- Meet HAA "housing development project" definition (⅔ residential square footage)
- Meet affordability: 7% ELI, 10% VLI, or 13% LI (statutory or local, whichever higher)
- Be on land zoned residential, mixed-use, or commercial
- Meet minimum 30 du/ac or local zoning minimum, whichever greater
- Limit average unit size to 1,750 net habitable sq ft

Projects exceeding 85ft must meet SB 35/SB 423 prevailing wage requirements.

## Enforcement Pipeline

```
Violation occurs
    ↓
HCD Technical Assistance letter (voluntary compliance request)
    ↓ (city given 30-60 days to respond)
HCD Notice of Violation
    ↓
HCD referral to Attorney General
    ↓
AG investigation → fines ($10K-$50K/month)
    ↓
Court-ordered compliance / rezoning
```

**Parallel track:** Private enforcement by CalHDF, YIMBY Law, Californians for Homeownership. These orgs sue directly under HAA without waiting for HCD pipeline.

**Coalition pressure track:** Multi-org coalitions (YIMBY Dems + CalHDF + YIMBY Law + CA YIMBY + Circulate + CFH) submit joint demand letters to MPOs and cities simultaneously — pre-litigation positioning that establishes the legal record before suits are filed.

## Penalty Calculator

### HAA Violation (Project Denial)

| Component | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Base penalty | $10,000 × number of units denied |
| Bad faith multiplier | 5x (= $50,000/unit) |
| Prior violations | Additional multiplier |
| Attorney's fees | Mandatory — paid to plaintiff |
| Appeal bond | Required if city appeals (La Cañada Flintridge: $14M bond ordered) |

**Example:** Deny 100-unit compliant project in bad faith = $5M minimum + attorney's fees + appeal bond.

### Housing Element Noncompliance

- Loss of Prohousing Designation (affects grant eligibility)
- Builder's Remedy activated (city loses ability to deny projects with ≥20% affordable)
- AB 1398: must complete rezoning within 1 year of deadline or face ministerial approval
- SB 35 streamlining triggered for cities behind RHNA

### SB 79 Noncompliance (after Jan 1, 2027)

- Presumption of HAA violation for denials in high-resource areas (§ 65912.157(m))
- Shifts burden of proof to city
- SANDAG TOD map carries rebuttable presumption of validity (§ 65912.160(f))

## Compliance Exposure Assessment

When evaluating a city's legal exposure, check these seven factors:

1. **RHNA progress** — % of lower-income obligation met at cycle midpoint. Below 50% = SB 35 triggered
2. **Housing Element status** — HCD-certified? If lapsed, Builder's Remedy active
3. **Prohousing Designation** — Has it? At risk of losing it?
4. **Recent denials** — Any compliant project denied? Each is a potential HAA lawsuit
5. **Downzoning since Jan 1, 2018** — SB 330 violation
6. **SB 79 compliance** — Correct tier classifications? Exemptions legally supportable?
7. **Pending litigation** — CalHDF, YIMBY Law, Californians for Homeownership actively suing?

**Cumulative exposure compounds.** A city with housing element noncompliance + project denials + SB 330 violations + SB 79 manipulation faces overlapping penalties, multiple plaintiff organizations, and AG enforcement simultaneously.

## The Coastal Act Tension

The Coastal Act (Pub. Resources Code §§ 30000-30900) creates genuine legal tension with state housing mandates:

- CCC retains discretionary authority over coastal development
- Can override density bonus waivers on coastal resource grounds
- New Commune v. Redondo Beach (2024): partial Builder's Remedy in Coastal Zone with limitations
- Kalnel Gardens v. City of LA (2016): Coastal Act initially subordinated Density Bonus; legislature later modified
- SB 79 collision with Coastal Act: 75-85% probability SB 79 prevails based on statutory structure and legislative intent

Cities use the Coastal Act as a shield to block density while appearing to comply with state law. The shield is weakening but not yet fully resolved.
