# Land-Use Policy Analysis

Framework for cutting through political framing to evaluate what land-use policy actually does vs what proponents claim it does.

## The Process

### Step 1. Ignore the Framing

Traditional land-use debate is fought on vibes: "neighborhood character," "community input," "responsible growth," "protecting quality of life." These phrases are marketing. Skip them entirely.

Instead ask:
- What does this policy **physically produce** on the ground? (units, lane-miles, pipe-feet, service calls)
- What does it **cost** to build and maintain? Over what lifecycle?
- What **revenue** does it generate per acre?
- **Who pays** and **who benefits**?

### Step 2. Go to the Actual Data

Start with peer-reviewed literature, not think tanks, not advocacy orgs, not pop-urbanism.

**Evidence hierarchy:**
1. Peer-reviewed meta-analyses and literature reviews (Ahlfeldt & Pietrostefani 2017: 102 studies, 202 elasticity estimates)
2. Peer-reviewed empirical studies with large samples (Goodman 2019: 30-year US county panel; Carruthers & Ulfarsson 2003: 283 metro counties)
3. Government-commissioned research with panel review (TCRP Report 74, Halifax Growth Scenarios)
4. Practitioner fiscal analyses where the math is transparent (revenue-per-acre calculations)
5. Think-tank reports from ideologically committed orgs — treat as advocacy, verify independently

**When contrarian work exists:** Check if it's peer-reviewed, who funded it, whether it's been specifically rebutted, and whether subsequent research confirmed or refuted it. One paper from 1997 doesn't outweigh 25 years of replicating studies. A policy advocate with no journal publications is not a counterweight to a meta-analysis.

**Don't false-balance.** The academic literature on sprawl costs, induced demand, and fiscal productivity of density is as one-sided as climate science. Presenting refuted contrarian work as "the other side" is misinformation, not balance.

See `/home/thomas/repos/civics/research/density-fiscal-literature-review.md` for the full evidence base on density and fiscal productivity.

### Step 3. Form a Position From the Evidence

Once you have the data, state what it shows. Plainly.

**What the fiscal literature shows:**
- Compact development generates 10-100x more revenue per acre than sprawl
- Suburban infrastructure costs more to serve per acre than it generates in revenue
- Urban cores cross-subsidize suburban areas, not the other way around
- New development looks profitable for 20-30 years; the replacement cycle at 30-50 years reveals the true cost
- The direction is not in dispute across the literature; the magnitude varies by study
- The U-shaped density-cost curve is real but the ascending right limb (very high density = expensive) kicks in at densities essentially no US city outside Manhattan reaches

**What the political structure shows:**
- In cities with district-based representation, suburban districts often outnumber urban ones
- The beneficiaries of cross-subsidy hold the votes to maintain it
- "Neighborhood character" protection and anti-density votes are revealed preferences for maintaining subsidized infrastructure
- State preemption (SB 9, SB 79, builder's remedy, HCD enforcement) exists because local political capture makes reform from within structurally impossible

### Step 4. Evaluate Political Actions Against Stated Goals

This is where the framework earns its keep. Take the politician's or agency's **stated goal**, then evaluate their **actual vote or action** against the evidence.

**The method:**
1. What did they say they want? (affordable housing, fiscal sustainability, safe streets, arts funding, Vision Zero)
2. What did they actually vote for / implement / cut?
3. Does the action advance the stated goal according to the evidence?
4. If not, who actually benefits from the action?

**Examples from San Diego / Oceanside:**

| Stated Goal | Actual Action | Evidence Says | Who Actually Benefits |
|-------------|---------------|---------------|----------------------|
| "Housing affordability" | Cap ADU bonus program, restrict density | Restricting supply raises prices (Econ 101, confirmed by Glaeser et al.) | Incumbent homeowners — property values protected |
| "Vision Zero" | Eliminate 12 complete streets engineers ($2.9M savings) | Complete streets reduce pedestrian/cyclist fatalities | Drivers in suburban districts — road maintenance budget preserved |
| "Fiscal sustainability" | Maintain sprawl infrastructure while cutting arts/parks | Sprawl is a net fiscal drain per the literature | Suburban homeowners — subsidized infrastructure maintained |
| "Community access" | Kill Balboa Park paid parking | Museums lose $11.8M in arts funding; attendance was already declining pre-parking | Single-family homeowners — free parking preserved, costs socialized |
| "SB 79 compliance" | Classify major transit hub as Tier 2, define "walking path" as continuous sidewalk | Exclude parcels from housing mandate using city's own infrastructure neglect | Existing property owners near transit — development blocked |
| "Protecting renters" | Classify Good Cause eviction as "rent control" for SB 79 exemption | Good Cause eviction ≠ rent stabilization under Costa-Hawkins | Landlords — properties exempted from upzoning |

### Step 5. Rebut Where Necessary

When the stated rationale doesn't match the evidence, say so directly. Not "there are concerns" — state the contradiction.

**Rebuttal patterns:**

- **"People chose suburbs, so suburbs are optimal"** → Revealed preference argument ignores massive policy distortions: highway subsidies, exclusionary zoning, parking mandates, FHA patterns, property tax structures. People chose from a distorted menu.

- **"Density is expensive to serve"** → True only above ~24,000 people/sq mi (Manhattan). For all practical US policy decisions, moving toward density *reduces* per-capita costs. The relevant range is entirely on the descending left limb of the U-shaped curve.

- **"We can't afford transit/complete streets"** → You can't afford not to. Car-dependent infrastructure is the fiscal drain. The $2.9M in complete streets engineers generates more long-term fiscal value than the suburban road maintenance it would be redirected to.

- **"Paid parking hurts access"** → Free parking is a subsidy. When the subsidy is removed, the question isn't "did attendance drop?" — it's "what's the baseline trend, and what's the counterfactual?" If arts funding gets cut $11.8M instead, that's the real access harm.

- **"This reflects community input"** → Community input in land-use is structurally captured by incumbent property owners who show up to meetings. Renters, future residents, and people priced out of the jurisdiction don't testify. "Community input" means "current homeowner preference."

- **"The walking path doesn't exist"** → The city chose not to build sidewalks, then uses that choice to exclude parcels from state housing mandates. Infrastructure neglect is not a basis for exemption from state law.

## Common Municipal Obstruction Patterns

| Pattern | How It Works | What Breaks It |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| **Weaponized neglect** | Don't build sidewalks → claim no "walking path" → exclude from transit housing mandate | Cross-reference with actual pedestrian usage, state definition of walking distance |
| **Tier manipulation** | Undercount trains to classify transit hub as lower tier | Train count data from multiple transit operators (they don't coordinate responses) |
| **Coastal Act shield** | Route density limits through CCC process to override state density bonus law | CCC's own analysis of housing production impacts |
| **Creative definitions** | Redefine statutory terms narrowly (Good Cause = rent control, walking path = paved sidewalk) | HCD guidance, legislative intent, plain statutory language |
| **Revenue misdirection** | Claim new revenue "reinvested in X" while it flows to General Fund first | Budget line-item analysis, IBA reports |
| **The Ponzi extension** | Approve new greenfield subdivision to fund maintenance of aging subdivision | Infrastructure lifecycle cost analysis, replacement schedule |
