# Authoritative Sources

Where to find data for housing policy analysis. Use these to verify claims rather than relying on city self-reporting.

|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| **HCD** | Housing Element reviews, RHNA allocations, Technical Assistance letters, enforcement actions, APR data, MPO advisories | [APR Dashboard](https://www.hcd.ca.gov/housing-open-data-tools/apr-dashboard) · [Open Data CSV/API](https://data.ca.gov/dataset/housing-element-annual-progress-report-apr-data-by-jurisdiction-and-year) |
| **Attorney General** | Civil penalties, NOVs, legal opinions (Builder's Remedy), enforcement suits | [Press releases](https://oag.ca.gov/media/news) · [Housing enforcement](https://oag.ca.gov/housing) |

## How to Use Sources in Analysis

1. **Start with HCD.** For any compliance question, HCD's dashboard and enforcement letters are the authoritative starting point.
2. **Verify transit data independently.** Don't trust city-reported train counts. Pull GTFS schedules and count yourself.
3. **Cross-reference enforcement org output.** CalHDF, YIMBY Law, and CFH publish demand letters and litigation filings. If they've written to a city, the city is on a litigation timeline.
4. **Use Legistar for vote records.** Staff reports contain the framing; vote records reveal the action. The gap between them is where analysis lives.
5. **Journalism is context, not evidence.** Use VOSD and CalMatters for timeline and political context, but ground legal and fiscal claims in statute and peer-reviewed literature.
