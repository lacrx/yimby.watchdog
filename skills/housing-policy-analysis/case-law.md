# Key Case Law

Court decisions that define how California housing law is enforced. Each entry includes the holding, practical impact, and how it applies to common municipal obstruction tactics.

## Builder's Remedy

### CalHDF v. City of La Cañada Flintridge (2024)

**Holding:** Builder's Remedy applies when housing element is noncompliant. Preliminary applications vest at submission and survive subsequent compliance.

**Practical impact:**
- First appellate Builder's Remedy victory
- $1.26M attorney fee settlement — CalHDF collects fees, self-funding future enforcement
- $14M appeal bond ordered — city abandoned appeal rather than post bond
- Establishes that BR vesting is irrevocable: a developer who files during noncompliance keeps BR protections even if city later cures defects

**Obstruction relevance:** Cities that delay housing element compliance risk BR applications that lock in permanently. The vesting rule creates a race condition — every day of noncompliance is a window for BR filings that cannot be undone.

### Shelby v. City of Goleta (2025)

**Holding:** Builder's Remedy applies to amended projects, not just original submissions.

**Practical impact:** Expanded BR scope. Developers can modify projects after filing without losing BR protections.

**Obstruction relevance:** Cities cannot defeat BR by forcing project modifications through the review process.

## Housing Accountability Act (HAA)

### Cal. Renters v. City of San Mateo (2021)

**Holding:** Post-SB 167 HAA enforcement standard established. Cities cannot use subjective design standards to deny planned housing.

**Practical impact:** Appellate precedent that vague "design" or "character" findings are insufficient basis for denial under HAA. Cities must cite specific, objective, pre-existing standards.

**Obstruction relevance:** Directly undercuts cities that use design review committees as delay/denial mechanisms.

### CaRLA v. City of Huntington Beach (2024 — Victory Alert)

**Holding:** Cities cannot use vague "goals and policies" findings, manufactured traffic/safety impacts, or charter-city sovereignty claims to deny housing under HAA. City staff approval creates "deemed consistent" status.

**Practical impact:**
- $600K settlement
- Established that subjective "spirit" and "vision" findings are pretextual
- Charter city status does not override state housing law

**Obstruction relevance:** Parallel to walking path fabrication, habitat exclusion, and manufactured safety findings. Courts reject pretextual denial rationales even when they sound plausible.

### CaRLA v. City of Los Altos (2020)

**Holding:** Cities cannot fabricate reasons to deny housing under SB 35/HAA.

**Practical impact:**
- $7M appeal bond ordered (14% of city budget)
- City conceded rather than post bond
- Established HAA bond precedent — makes delay-through-litigation financially untenable

**Obstruction relevance:** Direct parallel to fabricated walking paths, misclassified transit stops, and creative statutory definitions. Courts reject city-invented compliance obstacles.

### Martinez v. City of Clovis (2023)

**Holding:** Substantial compliance with Housing Element Law is a question of law. City self-certification is insufficient — only HCD or a court determines substantial compliance.

**Practical impact:** Cities cannot declare their own housing elements compliant and use that declaration to shield against enforcement.

**Obstruction relevance:** Prevents the "we're already compliant" defense when HCD has not certified.

## Coastal Act Intersection

### New Commune v. City of Redondo Beach (2024)

**Holding:** Partial Builder's Remedy applies in Coastal Zone, with limitations. Coastal Act provides some shield but does not completely override state housing mandates.

**Practical impact:** Opened the door for BR in coastal areas but didn't fully resolve the tension. Cities still have some Coastal Act defenses.

**Obstruction relevance:** The Coastal Act shield is weakening. Cities relying on CCC process to block density face increasing risk that courts will subordinate Coastal Act to housing mandates. The trend line favors housing law.

### Kalnel Gardens v. City of LA (2016)

**Holding:** Coastal Act initially subordinated Density Bonus Law.

**Practical impact:** Legislature subsequently modified the relationship. Ongoing tension between coastal preservation and housing production.

**Obstruction relevance:** This is the case coastal cities cite to justify blocking density. But it's pre-modification and the legal landscape has shifted significantly since.

## Enforcement Patterns

### CalHDF Litigation Trajectory

CalHDF (formerly CaRLA) follows a documented escalation pattern:

```
Warning letters → demand letters → pre-litigation positioning → lawsuit
```

Timeline from warning to litigation: typically ~6-12 months. Key data points:

| City | Warning | Lawsuit | Outcome |
|------|---------|---------|---------|
| La Cañada Flintridge | Letters | Filed | Won — $1.26M fees, $14M bond |
| Huntington Beach | Letters | Filed | Won — $600K settlement |
| Los Altos | Letters | Filed | Won — city conceded after $7M bond |
| San Francisco | Letters | Filed (2026) | Pending |
| Los Altos Hills | Letters | Filed | Pending |

**YIMBY Law litigation record:** 9 wins out of 10 lawsuits filed. Cities almost always lose HAA cases.

### What This Means for Analysis

When evaluating a city's legal exposure:

1. Check whether CalHDF, YIMBY Law, or CFH have sent letters to the city
2. If letters exist, the city is on a documented litigation timeline
3. CalHDF is self-funding through fee recoveries — each win funds the next suit
4. The litigation is not performative. These orgs sue and win consistently
5. A city receiving a violation letter and not responding is not buying time — it's accumulating penalties
