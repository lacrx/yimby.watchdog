# Density, Sprawl, and Fiscal Reality: Literature Review

*Compiled 2026-05-26. Evidence weighted by methodological quality, peer-review status, replication, and whether critiques have withstood scrutiny.*

---

## Summary

The peer-reviewed literature overwhelmingly finds that compact development costs less to serve and generates more revenue per acre than sprawl. The direction is not in dispute. The magnitude varies by study. The contrarian literature is thin, aging, and largely non-peer-reviewed.

---

## Peer-Reviewed Evidence

### Meta-Analyses and Literature Reviews

**Ahlfeldt & Pietrostefani (2017)** — "The Economic Effects of Density: A Synthesis"
- *SERC Discussion Paper 210, London School of Economics*
- 102 studies, 202 elasticity estimates, 15 outcome categories
- Key elasticities (Table 5):
  - Local public spending: **-14.4%** per doubling of density
  - Wages: **+4%**
  - Rent: **+21%**
  - VMT reduction: **-8.5%**
  - Non-car mode share: **+7%**
  - Crime: **+8.5%**
  - Energy use: **-11%**
- Higher-quality studies (SMS >= 3) show ~6% smaller elasticities — effect is real but best studies find slightly more conservative magnitudes
- Monetary equivalents in Table 6
- Critical nuance: density harms renters via scarcity rent (21% rent elasticity)

**Ewing & Hamidi (2015)** — "Compactness versus Sprawl: A Review of Recent Evidence from the United States"
- *Journal of Planning Literature*
- Comprehensive post-1997 review
- Conclusion: "the empirical evidence in favor of compact development is overwhelming"

### National/Regional Studies

**Burchell et al. (2002)** — TCRP Report 74: "Costs of Sprawl 2000"
- *Transportation Research Board (National Academies)*
- National 25-year scenario modeling, all US counties
- Key findings:
  - Water/sewer savings: **$12.6B**
  - Road savings: **$109.7B** (188,305 fewer lane-miles)
  - Property development savings: **$420B** (6.6%)
  - Annual fiscal improvement: **$4.2B/yr**
- Preface acknowledges "both arguments have merit" but concludes "more costs than benefits" to sprawl
- Legitimate critique: assumes linear density-cost relationship (Ladd found it's U-shaped). Also gaps in municipal employee compensation data
- Directional finding confirmed by subsequent research even if magnitude debatable

**Goodman (2019)** — "The Fiscal Impacts of Urban Sprawl: Evidence From U.S. County Areas"
- *Public Budgeting & Finance*, Vol. 39, No. 4, pp. 3-27
- US county panel, 1982-2012 (30 years of data)
- **Critical distinction: spatial extent vs. density**
  - Reducing developed land from 50th to 25th percentile: **-$60.86/capita**
  - Increasing density 25th to 50th percentile: **+$5.26/capita**
  - Ratio: **12:1** — the footprint of development matters far more than density per se
- Implication: a suburb of rowhouses on a compact footprint is nearly as efficient as mid-rise urban

**Carruthers & Ulfarsson (2003)** — "Urban Sprawl and the Cost of Public Services"
- *Environment and Planning B*, Vol. 30, No. 4, pp. 503-522
- 283 US metropolitan counties, 1982-1992
- 12 spending categories analyzed
- 1% density increase → **2-4% decrease** in police/education costs
- ~3% decrease across all 12 service categories
- No significant density impact on fire protection or solid waste

### International Studies

**Hortas-Rico & Sole-Olle (2010)** — "Does Urban Sprawl Increase the Costs of Providing Local Public Services?"
- *Urban Studies*, Vol. 47, No. 7, pp. 1513-1540
- 2,500 Spanish municipalities
- Twofold expansion of urbanized land increases:
  - Community facilities: **+11%**
  - Local police: **+9%**
  - Housing: **+8%**
  - Culture/sports: **+15%**
- Average per capita cost increase due to sprawl: **2.3%**

**Halifax Growth Scenarios (2009)**
- Halifax Regional Municipality, 4 scenarios, 2009-2031
- Most compact scenario (B) saves **$3.07B** vs trend
- All scenarios: municipal costs exceed revenues by 2x+
- Scenario B saves **$715M net** for municipality
- "New residential developments do not pay their way" — but compact ones lose far less

**Sustainable Prosperity (2013)** — Canadian municipal case studies
- Edmonton: **$4B fiscal loss** over 60 years for 17 of 40 developments
- Calgary: **$11B capital savings** from compact growth
- Kitchener: suburban service charges **74-157% higher**
- Water/wastewater O&M: up to **2x** in low-density areas

**Lower Austria (2019)** — *PMC*
- Municipality of Michelhausen, 20-year model
- Low-density (91.5% detached): **-€1.93M at 5 years**, barely breaks even at 20 years
- Compact (76% row houses): **-€1.05M at 5 years, +€1.82M surplus at 20 years**
- Compact scenario accommodated **60% more inhabitants** on same land

**Vermeiren et al. (2021)** — Flanders, Belgium
- *Land Use Policy*
- Curbing sprawl saves **€246-383M/yr** in infrastructure, **€1.16-1.97B/yr** in mobility costs

### The U-Shaped Density-Cost Curve

**Ladd (1992)** — "Population Growth, Density, and the Costs of Providing Public Services"
- *Urban Studies*, Vol. 29, No. 2, pp. 273-295
- 247 large US county areas, 1985 data
- Found quadratic (U-shaped) relationship between density and per capita expenditure
- **Cost-minimizing density: ~250 residents/sq mi**
- Very low-density counties: **14% above** optimal
- Very high-density counties (24,000+/sq mi): **43% above** optimal
- Policy relevance: the ascending right limb (density too high) applies essentially only to Manhattan. Virtually all US policy decisions — including everything in San Diego — fall on the descending left limb where more density = lower costs

**IDB Working Paper** — Latin American municipalities (Brazil, Chile, Ecuador, Mexico)
- Optimal spending at ~9,000 residents/sq km (~23,300/sq mi)
- 85% of municipalities in sample were below optimal density

**Holcombe & Williams (2008)** — *Public Finance Review*
- 487 US municipal governments with population >50,000
- No significant relationship between density and per capita total spending for cities under 500,000
- Infrastructure expenditures decline with density for smaller cities

## The Contrarian Literature: Assessed by Weight

### Gordon & Richardson (1997) — "Are Compact Cities a Desirable Planning Goal?"

- *Journal of the American Planning Association*, Vol. 63, No. 1, pp. 95-106
- **Peer-reviewed: Yes.** JAPA is APA's flagship journal. ~1,200 citations
- Published as deliberate point-counterpoint; Ewing's rebuttal "Is Los Angeles-Style Sprawl Desirable?" ran in same issue
- **Legitimate academic contribution that catalyzed important debate**
- Core argument: revealed preference (people chose suburbs → suburbs are optimal)
- Critiques:
  - Does not account for policy distortions shaping choices (highway subsidies, exclusionary zoning, FHA patterns, parking mandates)
  - Uses commute time as primary transport metric, ignoring VMT, household costs, externalities
  - Conflates suburbanization (living outside center) with sprawl (specific low-density car-dependent pattern)
  - LA commute-time claims have not held up
- **Status:** Real paper, real academics, arguments substantially refuted by subsequent 25 years of empirical work

### Wendell Cox (Demographia, Heritage Foundation, Heartland Institute)

- **No peer-reviewed publications** in academic journals
- No tenured/tenure-track academic position (MBA from Pepperdine)
- Affiliations: Heritage Foundation, Heartland Institute, Cato Institute, Reason Foundation
- Best known for annual Demographia Housing Affordability Survey (not peer-reviewed, criticized for using median-multiple without adjusting for housing quality/size/location)
- **Status:** Policy advocate, not academic researcher. No meaningful peer-reviewed record. Citing Cox in academic context is equivalent to citing a lobbyist

### Cox & Utt (2004) — Heritage Foundation "The Costs of Sprawl Reconsidered"

- **Not peer-reviewed.** Heritage Foundation policy brief
- Central methodological error: compares per-capita municipal spending across municipalities without controlling for service scope, quality, or cost of living
- A city providing police+fire+transit+libraries will obviously spend more per capita than a bedroom suburb that freeloads on county services
- Ignores infrastructure capital costs per unit entirely (where compact development's advantage is strongest)
- **Litman's point-by-point rebuttal (2015)** identifies specific misrepresentations:
  - Cox claims Hsieh & Moretti criticize urban containment; they actually recommend Smart Growth policies
  - Cox claims Saks criticizes density; she actually criticizes sprawl-inducing regulations
  - Cox's $2T lost productivity estimate should be *added to* sprawl costs, not subtracted
- **Status:** Advocacy document from ideologically committed organization. Thoroughly refuted. Methods credibly challenged on multiple specific grounds

### Glaeser & Kahn (2004) — "Sprawl and Urban Growth"

- *Handbook of Regional and Urban Economics*, Vol. 4 (Elsevier). Prestigious invited chapter
- **Often misrepresented by sprawl defenders.** Actually says:
  - Sprawl is "the inexorable product of car-based living"
  - "The primary social problem associated with sprawl": equity — "some people are left behind because they do not earn enough to afford the cars"
  - Acknowledges congestion externalities warrant policy intervention
- Glaeser subsequently wrote *Triumph of the City* (2011) explicitly arguing dense cities are greener, more productive, more innovative
- **Status:** Citing Glaeser as pro-sprawl is a misrepresentation of his work

## Where Genuine Uncertainty Exists

1. **Magnitude, not direction.** Effect sizes vary by study (Burchell's $227B vs. Ahlfeldt's more conservative elasticities in higher-quality studies). The direction is not in question

2. **The U-shaped curve is real** but the right limb (costs rising at very high density) applies only to densities essentially no US city outside Manhattan reaches. Irrelevant for policy decisions in San Diego

3. **Density harms renters through scarcity rent.** Ahlfeldt: 21% rent elasticity. Density's fiscal benefits accrue to the jurisdiction; housing cost burdens fall on tenants. This is a distributional concern — an argument for coupling density with tenant protections, not against density

4. **Spatial extent vs. density (Goodman 2019).** How spread out development is matters 12x more than how dense it is. A compact suburb of rowhouses may be nearly as efficient as mid-rise urban. The real fiscal killer is spreading low-density development over vast areas of infrastructure

5. **Housing affordability in supply-constrained markets.** Some land-use regulations restrict supply and raise prices (Glaeser). But the solution is removing barriers to infill density, not encouraging greenfield sprawl

## Applied: San Diego

San Diego's suburban districts cost more to serve per acre than they generate in revenue. This is not pop-urbanism — it is the consistent finding of peer-reviewed literature across multiple countries, methodologies, and decades.

The ADU Bonus Program rollback (suburban districts voting to restrict housing production during a housing crisis and $118M budget deficit) is fiscally self-defeating. Those districts are voting to protect a development pattern that requires cross-subsidy from the urban core.

Cutting 12 complete streets engineers ($2.9M savings) while claiming Vision Zero goals compounds this: the investments that would make dense, walkable neighborhoods more productive are being sacrificed to maintain a budget structurally strained by the cost of servicing low-density areas.

The 6-of-9 suburban district majority is making a revealed-preference argument — "we like what we have" — that, per 30 years of fiscal research, the rest of the city is paying for.
