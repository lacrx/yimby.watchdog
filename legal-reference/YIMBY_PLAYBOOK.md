## SECTION 1: CALIFORNIA HOUSING LAW QUICK REFERENCE

### 1.1 Housing Accountability Act (HAA) — Gov. Code § 65589.5

**What it requires:** Cities may not deny, reduce density of, or render infeasible housing development projects that comply with objective zoning and general plan standards, unless they can make very specific written findings related to health and safety. The HAA must be "interpreted and implemented to afford the fullest possible weight to the interest of, and the approval and provision of, housing." (§ 65589.5(a)(2)(L).)

**Key provisions:**
- § 65589.5(d): Limits grounds for disapproval to objective standards or specific health/safety findings
- § 65589.5(f)(4): When general plan and zoning conflict, general plan land use controls
- § 65589.5(h)(6): Defines "disapproval" broadly — includes: (A) express denial by vote; (B) failure to meet Permit Streamlining Act deadlines; (C) causing unnecessary delays to increase project costs; (D) determining application incomplete without written submittal checklist basis; (E) failing to comply with SB 330's 5-hearing rule; (F) wrongfully determining applicant lost vested rights; (G) refusing to process a Builder's Remedy application
- § 65589.5(j): HAA violation penalties — minimum $10,000 per unit, attorney's fees
- § 65589.5(k): If city fails to comply with court order, fines multiply by 5x for bad faith
- § 65589.5(l): Prior HAA violations in same planning period multiply fines further
- § 65589.5(m): If city appeals HAA judgment, court must order appeal bond (La Cañada Flintridge: $14 million bond ordered, city dismissed appeal rather than pay)

**What a violation looks like:** Denial of a compliant project on subjective grounds ("doesn't fit neighborhood character," "too dense," "design concerns"); applying standards not in effect when application was deemed complete; creating incompleteness determinations not supported by written submittal checklist; imposing more than 5 hearings on a compliant project.

**Who enforces:** Private applicants via lawsuit; YIMBY Law; CalHDF; HCD via referral to Attorney General (§ 65585(j)); AG directly.

**Remedy:** Mandatory approval order; attorney's fees; $10,000+/unit minimum fines; potential 5x multiplier for bad faith.

### 1.2 SB 330 — Housing Crisis Act (Gov. Code § 66300 et seq.)

**What it requires (through 2030):**
- § 66300(b)(1): No reduction in "residential intensity" below the level permitted as of January 1, 2018 — prohibits downzoning, new height limits, new setbacks, reduced lot coverage, reduced density
- § 66300(d): Demolition of protected units (rent-controlled, price-restricted, occupied by low/very-low income in last 5 years, Ellis Act units) requires replacement in-kind
- § 65905.5(a): Maximum 5 public hearings on any project compliant with zoning and general plan; city must decide within that limit
- § 65941.1: Preliminary application process — locks in applicable ordinances, policies, and standards at time of submission, including Builder's Remedy availability

**What a violation looks like:** Adopting a new design review overlay that reduces effective density; scheduling hearing after hearing to delay without reaching a decision; adopting new parking minimums or setback requirements; attempting to require more than 5 hearings on a code-compliant project.

**Who enforces:** Private applicants; HAU; YIMBY Law; CalHDF.

**Remedy:** HAA penalties; writ of mandate compelling approval.

---

### 1.3 SB 35 — Streamlined Ministerial Approval (Gov. Code § 65913.4)

**What it requires:** Ministerial (by-right, no discretionary review, CEQA-exempt) approval for multifamily projects that: (a) include specified affordable unit percentages; (b) meet objective zoning standards; (c) are in cities that have not met their RHNA allocation pace for the relevant income category; (d) meet labor standards above 85 feet. Amended by SB 423 (2023) to extend and expand.

**What a violation looks like:** Requiring discretionary review, design commission hearings, or environmental review for an SB 35-qualifying project; applying subjective standards; imposing conditions not authorized under ministerial review.

**Who enforces:** Private applicants; HCD; YIMBY Law.

**Remedy:** Writ of mandate; HAA penalties if denial also violates HAA.

### 1.5 ADU Law — Gov. Code §§ 65852.2 and 66310–66342

**What it requires:** Cities must ministerially approve ADUs and JADUs that meet objective standards. Prohibits: replacement parking requirements when garage is converted; owner-occupancy requirements (until 2025); setback requirements greater than 4 feet for rear/side; minimum ADU size less than 150 sq ft; design standards that would prevent construction of a compliant ADU.

**What a violation looks like:** Requiring design review for an ADU; imposing parking requirements when law prohibits them; requiring owner-occupancy; denying ADU by citing "neighborhood character"; imposing setback, height, or size standards beyond statutory maxima; treating deed restrictions as a basis to deny ADU where no such restriction is legally enforceable.

**Who enforces:** HCD Housing Accountability Unit (HAU) — has issued extensive enforcement letters on ADU violations; private applicants; YIMBY Law.

**Remedy:** HCD notice of violation → mandatory correction or referral to AG; writ of mandate.

### 1.6 Housing Element Law — Gov. Code §§ 65580–65589.9

**What it requires:** Every city must adopt a housing element (updated each planning cycle, currently 6th cycle 2023–2031) that: (a) inventories adequate sites to accommodate RHNA allocation at all income levels; (b) removes governmental constraints to housing development (§ 65583(c)(3)); (c) affirmatively furthers fair housing; (d) is reviewed and certified by HCD (§ 65585); (e) is adopted following required procedures.

**Key rule on compliance:** Substantial compliance is a question of law — only HCD or a court can determine it (AB 1886, adding §§ 65585.03 and 65589.55). "Self-certification" by a city (adopting without HCD certification) does not establish compliance. HCD certification creates a rebuttable presumption of compliance (§ 65589.3); adoption without certification carries no such presumption.

**What a violation looks like:** Failing to adopt a housing element by statutory deadline; adopting a housing element that HCD has found noncompliant; listing inadequate or infeasible sites; failing to remove identified constraints; rushing adoption to defeat Builder's Remedy applications.

**Who enforces:** HCD (can decertify and refer to AG); CalHDF; YIMBY Law; private parties via writ of mandate (§ 65587). Note 35-day CEQA statute of limitations for challenges on CEQA grounds.

**Remedy:** Court-ordered rezoning; loss of housing element certification (triggering Builder's Remedy); AG intervention.

### 1.7 Density Bonus Law — Gov. Code § 65915

**What it requires:** When a developer includes affordable units (thresholds vary by income level and unit count), the city must grant: (a) a density bonus above base zoning; (b) reduced parking; (c) incentives and concessions (reduced development standards); (d) waivers of development standards that would prevent achieving the density bonus. City may not apply standards that physically prevent the bonus density from being built.

**Key interactions:** Builder's Remedy projects are eligible for density bonus (AB 1893). SB 79 projects may use SB 79 density as the base for density bonus calculations. However, for SB 79 projects, cities are NOT required to grant height waivers beyond SB 79 statutory limits.

**What a violation looks like:** Denying a concession without making required written findings that the concession would cause specific, adverse impacts; applying a development standard that physically prevents the bonus density; requiring waivers to go through discretionary design review.

**Who enforces:** Private applicants; HCD; YIMBY Law.

**Remedy:** Writ of mandate; HAA penalties if denial also violates HAA.

### 1.8 Builder's Remedy — Gov. Code § 65589.5(d)(5), (h)(11); amended by AB 1893 (eff. Jan. 1, 2025)

**What triggers it:** City's housing element is NOT in substantial compliance (as determined by HCD or a court). Compliance is determined only by HCD or a court — not by the city itself.

**What it does:** During the period of noncompliance, a city cannot use its zoning or general plan to disapprove a housing development project that includes either: (a) 20% affordable to lower-income households (pre-AB 1893 standard, available to applications vested before Jan. 1, 2025); or (b) under AB 1893's new framework, meets revised density and affordability standards, including eligibility for density bonus law.

**How an application vests:** Submit a complete SB 330 preliminary application (§ 65941.1) while the city is out of compliance. The preliminary application locks in applicable law — including Builder's Remedy availability — as of the date of submission. (§ 65589.5(o).) This was confirmed in *CalHDF v. City of La Cañada Flintridge* (Super. Ct. L.A. County, 2024) and *Shelby Family Partnership v. City of Goleta* (Super. Ct. S.B. County, 2025).

**What counts as "disapproval":** Broadly interpreted. Includes: express denial; failure to take final action within deadlines; refusing to process the application; determining application is incomplete when a reasonable person would conclude otherwise; any action that "concludes the processing of the project as proposed." (*Goleta* case.) AB 1893 codified this broad definition at § 65589.5(h)(6).

**What a violation looks like:** Refusing to process a Builder's Remedy preliminary application; treating the application as incomplete without written checklist basis; adopting a local ordinance or alternative that purports to defeat vested Builder's Remedy rights; claiming the housing element is "self-certified" and therefore compliant when HCD has not certified it.

**Penalties for violation:** $10,000/unit minimum; 5x multiplier for bad faith/failure to comply with court order; mandatory appeal bond if city appeals judgment.

**Who enforces:** Private applicants (CalHDF has won the first Builder's Remedy court victory); AG (notified by HCD under § 65585(j)); YIMBY Law. AG has automatic right to intervene in third-party enforcement actions (§ 65585.01).

## SECTION 2: RED FLAG PATTERNS

When analyzing meeting minutes, treat the following as probable indicators of state law violation or bad faith obstruction:

**2.1 Post-Hoc Findings**
Staff or council manufacture findings *after* deciding to deny, rather than applying standards *before* reviewing the project. Red flags: findings that precisely track opponent testimony rather than staff analysis; findings citing standards not in city's written code; "health and safety" findings without supporting technical evidence; findings appearing for the first time in the denial resolution that were not mentioned in the staff report.

**2.2 Design Review as Delay**
Using aesthetic, architectural, or "compatibility" review as a mechanism to delay or defeat projects. Red flags: conditioning approval on changes that reduce density; requiring multiple design commission hearings beyond what written code requires; applying subjective standards ("fits neighborhood character," "appropriate massing," "compatible materials") to projects that are otherwise code-compliant; design conditions that would require project redesign eliminating affordable units.

**2.3 CEQA Abuse**
Using environmental review as a substantive barrier rather than a procedural requirement. Red flags: ordering an EIR for a project that qualifies for a categorical or statutory exemption; scoping environmental review to include traffic, aesthetics, or neighborhood character impacts when project is infill near transit; using CEQA process to add years of delay and cost; citing CEQA as a basis to deny a project when CEQA only governs procedure, not substantive approval. Note: CEQA abuse is especially common in coastal cities where the Coastal Commission retains parallel discretionary authority.

**2.4 Density Caps and Non-Objective Standards**
Any attempt to impose a density lower than what zoning allows, or to apply a standard that a reasonable person could not consistently apply. Red flags: "maximum" unit counts below what zoning permits; discretionary findings on "neighborhood scale" or "community character"; FAR or height limits applied inconsistently project-to-project; "design guidelines" that function as binding standards but were never adopted through proper zoning ordinance process.

**2.5 "Neighborhood Character" Justification**
The phrase "neighborhood character" is not an objective standard and cannot be a lawful basis for denial under the HAA. Red flags: council members citing existing single-family character as a reason to oppose multifamily; staff findings referencing "compatibility with surrounding context" without citing a specific, adopted, measurable standard; any finding that amounts to "this neighborhood has always been low-density and should stay that way."

**2.6 Parking Minimums Above State Law**
State law prohibits parking minimums for: ADUs in certain circumstances; projects within ½ mile of transit using SB 35 or SB 79; density bonus projects when state minimums apply. Red flags: conditions of approval requiring parking spaces beyond state minimums; city imposing parking requirements as a "concession" trade in density bonus negotiations; parking requirements imposed through design review rather than zoning code.

**2.7 Rushed Local Alternatives to State Mandates**
Cities sometimes adopt local ordinances, specific plans, or "alternative plans" designed to defeat state mandates before they take effect. Red flags: emergency ordinances passed immediately before a state law's effective date; "TOD alternative plans" under SB 79 that reduce capacity below what SB 79 would allow without HCD approval; housing element amendments adopted without HCD certification in a rush to defeat pending Builder's Remedy applications; adoption of a non-compliant housing element "without changes" after HCD rejection, to claim compliance.

**2.8 Left-NIMBY Pattern (Pro-Tenant + Anti-Density)**
A common coastal California pattern: council members express genuine concern for existing tenants and affordable housing while simultaneously opposing new market-rate or mixed-income development. Red flags: invoking tenant displacement concerns to oppose projects that trigger no displacement (vacant sites, commercial sites); requiring affordability percentages so high that projects become infeasible (ask: has a feasibility analysis been conducted?); supporting rent control expansions while opposing new supply; using "community benefit agreements" or excessive inclusionary requirements to make projects infeasible; treating market-rate housing as inherently harmful to affordability rather than supply-and-demand complementary.

### 3.2 Escalation Ladder for Local Advocates

**Step 1 — Public Comment on the Record**
Every statement made at a public hearing is part of the administrative record. Cite specific code sections. State the legal standard the city must apply. Identify the specific finding the city has failed to make. Even if you lose, you are building the record for litigation.

**Step 2 — Written Letter to Decision-Makers Before the Hearing**
Submit a written comment citing: (a) the applicable statute; (b) the objective standard the project meets; (c) the legal standard for denial; (d) the consequences of unlawful denial (HAA penalties, litigation). Letters create paper trails and may later support attorney's fees claims.

**Step 3 — HCD Complaint**
File via the HAU portal (hcd.ca.gov/hau/portal). Include: jurisdiction name, date of action, specific statute violated, description of what occurred, any supporting documents (staff report, meeting minutes, denial resolution). HCD will issue a Technical Assistance Letter or Notice of Violation. This creates official state-level pressure and may trigger AG referral.

**Step 4 — CalHDF/YIMBY Law Referral**
Contact CalHDF (hi@calhdf.org) or submit to YIMBY Law (yimbylaw.org/submit-a-project). These organizations are actively monitoring for enforcement opportunities. Provide: meeting minutes, staff report, denial resolution, project description, any prior HCD correspondence.

**Step 5 — Litigation**
Writ of mandate under Code Civ. Proc. §§ 1085 or 1094.5. HAA cases: mandatory attorney's fees if plaintiff prevails. Note statute of limitations: 90 days for most land use actions; 35 days for CEQA challenges. Time is critical — do not delay in escalating.

## SECTION 4: STRONG TOWNS FISCAL LENS

When evaluating any infrastructure spending decision or development approval, apply these four questions:

**Q1: What is the tax revenue per acre generated by this land use, and how does it compare to the infrastructure costs it requires?**
A surface parking lot, single-story retail pad, or large-lot single-family home generates far less tax revenue per acre than multifamily infill. Approving low-productivity land uses while citing infrastructure costs as a reason to deny density inverts the fiscal logic. The question is not "how much does infrastructure cost?" but "does the development generate enough return to pay for its infrastructure lifecycle costs?"

**Q2: Are infrastructure costs being treated as investment (requiring return) or consumption (ongoing expense)?**
Infrastructure is investment spending — a new road, pipe, or utility extension must generate enough tax base to cover its ongoing costs AND the eventual replacement cost. When cities approve sprawl-pattern development with long infrastructure runs and low density, they are deferring enormous future liabilities. When they deny infill multifamily that would use existing infrastructure more efficiently, they are making the fiscal problem worse.

**Q3: Who is being asked to subsidize whom?**
In coastal California cities, existing homeowners often receive heavily subsidized infrastructure services (streets, water, sewer, parks) funded in part by development fees from new residents who are denied the right to live at sufficient density to make the math work. Ask: is the city requiring new development to pay for infrastructure that existing residents already use at below-cost rates?

**Q4: Does this decision increase or decrease the city's long-term fiscal resilience?**
Approving dense infill near transit increases tax base per acre, reduces per-capita infrastructure cost, reduces vehicle miles traveled (and associated road wear), and increases the city's ability to fund services without raising taxes. Denying infill in favor of "neighborhood preservation" does the opposite. Any staff report that approves low-density uses or denies high-density infill without addressing long-term fiscal productivity should be treated as incomplete.

## SECTION 5: SB 79 SPECIFIC ANALYSIS GUIDE

### 5.1 What the Law Requires

SB 79 (Wiener, 2025), signed October 10, 2025, effective July 1, 2026 for most cities, overrides local zoning to allow multifamily residential development near qualifying transit stops. It is not a streamlining law by itself — it is an upzoning law that creates new base densities that other laws (SB 35/SB 423, HAA, Density Bonus Law) can then operate on.

**Tier 1 TOD stops** (heavy rail: BART, Caltrain, LA Metro B/D Lines; commuter rail with 72+ trains/day):
- Adjacent to stop: 75 ft height, 120 du/ac, 3.5 FAR (+ intensifier: +20 ft, +40 du/ac, +1.0 FAR if immediately adjacent)
- Within ¼ mile: 75 ft, 120 du/ac, 3.5 FAR
- ¼ to ½ mile (cities 35,000+ pop.): 65 ft, 100 du/ac, 3.0 FAR

**Tier 2 TOD stops** (light rail: SF Muni Metro, VTA, SacRT; commuter rail 48–71 trains/day; qualifying BRT):
- Within ¼ mile: 65 ft, 100 du/ac, 3.0 FAR
- ¼ to ½ mile (cities 35,000+ pop.): 55 ft, 80 du/ac, 2.5 FAR

These standards supersede contrary local zoning. Applies only in counties with 16+ passenger rail stations.

**Affordability requirements:** 7% extremely low income, OR 10% very low income, OR 13% lower income (or local requirement if higher). Projects of 10 units or fewer are exempt from state affordability requirements.

**Labor standards:** Projects over 85 feet must meet SB 423 prevailing wage/skilled and trained workforce requirements.

**Displacement protections:** Cannot require demolition of rent-stabilized units (3+ units) or multifamily housing with tenants in the last 7 years.

**HAA interaction:** A qualifying SB 79 project is deemed consistent with local standards for HAA purposes. Beginning January 1, 2027, denial of an SB 79 project in a "high-resource area" creates a presumption of HAA violation.

**SB 35 interaction:** SB 79 projects can qualify for ministerial CEQA-exempt approval under SB 35/SB 423 at only 10% very low income (rental) or 10% low income (for-sale) — a significant reduction from normal SB 35 thresholds.

**Density Bonus interaction:** SB 79 density becomes the base density for density bonus calculations. However, cities are NOT required to grant height waivers above SB 79 statutory limits (unlike normal density bonus law).
