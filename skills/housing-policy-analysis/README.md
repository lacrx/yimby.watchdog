# Housing Policy Analysis Skill

A framework for evaluating California municipal housing policy by cutting through political framing to determine what policy actually does, who it benefits, and whether it advances or obstructs stated goals.

## When to Use

- Evaluating a city council vote on housing, zoning, or land use
- Assessing a city's compliance with state housing mandates (RHNA, SB 79, HAA, Housing Element Law)
- Analyzing whether a politician's stated goals match their actual votes
- Identifying municipal obstruction patterns disguised as legitimate policy
- Estimating a city's legal exposure from housing law violations
- Evaluating fiscal claims about density, infrastructure, or development patterns

## How This Skill Works

This is a *methodology*, not a lookup table. The files below teach you how to think about housing policy, not just what to know. Follow this order:

### 1. Learn the Method → [methodology.md](methodology.md)
The 5-step analytical framework. Start here every time. This teaches you to ignore framing, find evidence, form positions, evaluate actions against stated goals, and rebut false claims.

### 2. Know the Law → [ca-housing-law.md](ca-housing-law.md)
California housing statutes, enforcement pipeline, and penalty structure. Use this to assess legal compliance and exposure. Includes SB 79 tier classification, HAA penalties, Builder's Remedy triggers, and the HCD enforcement pipeline.

### 3. Know the Cases → [case-law.md](case-law.md)
Key court decisions that define how the law is enforced. Each case includes the holding, its practical impact, and how it applies to common municipal obstruction tactics.

### 4. Detect Obstruction → [obstruction-patterns.md](obstruction-patterns.md)
A taxonomy of municipal evasion tactics — how cities violate state housing law while maintaining plausible deniability. Each pattern includes the mechanism, how to detect it, what legal vulnerability it creates, and real-world examples.

### 5. Use the Evidence → [evidence-base.md](evidence-base.md)
The peer-reviewed fiscal and planning literature. Use this to evaluate claims about density costs, sprawl benefits, infrastructure spending, and revenue productivity. Includes the evidence hierarchy, key findings, the contrarian literature (assessed), and rebuttal patterns.

### 6. Find the Data → [sources.md](sources.md)
Authoritative data sources — state agencies, enforcement organizations, academic research, legal analysis, journalism, and open datasets. Use these to verify claims rather than relying on city self-reporting.

## How to Apply This Skill

For any policy question:

1. **Strip the framing.** Identify what the policy physically produces (units, lane-miles, revenue/acre). Ignore "neighborhood character," "community input," "responsible growth."

2. **Find the evidence.** Start with peer-reviewed literature, not advocacy. Use the evidence hierarchy in [evidence-base.md](evidence-base.md).

3. **State what the data shows.** Plainly. No false balance. If the literature is one-sided, say so.

4. **Evaluate the action against the stated goal.** Politicians say "affordable housing" but vote to cap density. The evidence says restricting supply raises prices. State the contradiction.

5. **Check for obstruction patterns.** Compare the city's actions against [obstruction-patterns.md](obstruction-patterns.md). If the tactic matches a known pattern, name it and cite the legal vulnerability.

6. **Estimate legal exposure.** Use [ca-housing-law.md](ca-housing-law.md) to calculate penalties and identify which enforcement organizations are likely to act.

## Key Principle

**Don't false-balance.** The academic literature on sprawl costs, induced demand, and fiscal productivity of density is as one-sided as climate science. Presenting refuted contrarian work as "the other side" is misinformation, not balance. State what the evidence shows.
